Two worlds of Optimal Transport

For twenty years OT has evolved along two nearly separate tracks with no shared yardstick.

geometry Wasserstein geometry

  • Wasserstein distance \(W_2(\mu,\nu)\)
  • Geodesics & interpolations
  • Barycenters; gradient flows
  • Bures–Wasserstein manifold

What metric structure does OT induce?

computation Computational OT

  • Sinkhorn (entropic regularization)
  • Sliced \(\mathrm{SW}_2\) (random projections)
  • Low-rank & Minibatch OT
  • Neural OT (two-ICNN Makkuva)

How do we scale OT to millions of points?

Each community optimizes for its own goal. There is no unified measure of how well a fast approximate solution preserves the geometry of the true OT plan.

Kantorovich duality — a 2-minute recap

Two equivalent views of the same problem. Their gap is our central object.

Primal — transport plan

$$\mathrm{OT}^\star=\min_{\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)}\;\mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\pi}\bigl[c(x,y)\bigr]$$

Any feasible coupling \(\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)\) gives upper bound \(U\).

Dual — potential functions

$$\mathrm{OT}^\star=\max_{f\oplus g\,\leq\, c}\;\int f\,d\mu+\int g\,d\nu$$

Any dual-feasible pair \((f,g)\in\Phi_c\) gives lower bound \(L\).

Strong duality: both optima equal \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\). Any primal \(+\) any dual gives a certified bracket \([L,U]\) without ever computing \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\).

The missing quantity: the duality gap

The gap \(G=U-L\) is a computable certificate of distance to the true optimum — hover or click each term to explore.

Duality gap \(G\)
Certifies both primal and dual error without needing \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\). \(G=0\) iff both bounds are tight (optimality).
Upper bound \(U\)
Cost of any feasible transport plan \(\pi\in\Pi(\mu,\nu)\). Any approximate map gives a valid \(U\).
Lower bound \(L\)
Value of any dual-feasible potentials \((f,g)\) satisfying \(f(x)+g(y)\leq c(x,y)\).

The gap certifies both errors simultaneously

$$0 \;\leq\; U-\mathrm{OT}^\star \;\leq\; G$$
$$0 \;\leq\; \mathrm{OT}^\star-L \;\leq\; G$$

$$G\;=\;\underbrace{(U-\mathrm{OT}^\star)}_{\text{primal excess}}+\underbrace{(\mathrm{OT}^\star-L)}_{\text{dual deficit}}$$

Both errors are simultaneously controlled by \(G\). No ground truth \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\) is needed.

Measuring \(G\) is sufficient to certify accuracy. A small gap means both the primal plan and the dual potentials are near-optimal.

Every method controls one side of \(G\)

relaxation Raises \(L\) — shrinks dual deficit

  • Sliced \(\mathrm{SW}_2\): average 1-D dual values over random projections
  • Semi-dual: optimize one potential analytically, fix the other
  • Neural potentials: parametric \(f_\theta,g_\phi\) via minimax training
  • Dual OT: direct gradient ascent on dual objective

These push \(L\) toward \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\) from below.

restriction Lowers \(U\) — shrinks primal excess

  • Low-rank OT: restrict \(\pi\) to rank-\(r\) couplings \(\alpha\beta^\top\)
  • Minibatch OT: subsample \(m\ll n\) points, solve exactly on minibatch
  • Map rounding: round a Brenier-map push-forward to a feasible coupling
  • Free-support barycenters: restrict support to \(k\) particles

These pull \(U\) toward \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\) from above.

The duality principle: an approximate-OT method either raises \(L\) (relaxation), lowers \(U\) (restriction), or both. Only the latter gives a two-sided certified gap.

Why fast OT usually loses Wasserstein geometry

Gaussian ground truth \(n=3{,}000\), \(d=16\), 5 seeds. Sliced plateaus; certified methods trade cost for accuracy.
The sliced floor is structural — no amount of extra projections will beat Theorem 1. Certification requires both bounds to converge.

Theorem 1 — Exact deficit for shared covariance

For any two Gaussians with the same covariance, sliced OT loses a precise, computable fraction of geometry.

Theorem 1 (i) — Common covariance (exact)

Let \(\mu=\mathcal{N}(a,\Sigma)\), \(\nu=\mathcal{N}(b,\Sigma)\) with any SPD \(\Sigma\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\), \(m=a-b\). Then:

$$\frac{W_2^2(\mu,\nu)-\mathrm{SW}_2^2(\mu,\nu)}{W_2^2(\mu,\nu)}\;=\;1-\frac{1}{d}$$

The deficit is exactly \(1-1/d\), independent of the shift direction, magnitude, or covariance shape.

Proof idea

Project to direction \(\theta\): 1-D \(W_2^2=(\theta^\top m)^2\) and \(\mathcal B^2=0\) (same covariance). Average over the unit sphere: \(\mathbb{E}_\theta[(\theta^\top m)^2]=\|m\|^2/d\). So \(\mathrm{SW}_2^2=\|m\|^2/d\), giving the exact ratio.

Why this matters

Previous results assumed \(\Sigma=I\). Theorem 1(i) kills the "isotropic only" critique: the floor is universal — stretched, rotated, or correlated Gaussians all lose exactly \(1-1/d\).

At \(d=16\): deficit = 93.75%. Sliced retains only 6.25% of Wasserstein geometry.

Theorem 1 — Anisotropic lower bound

Theorem 1 (ii) — Anisotropic \(\Sigma_0\neq\Sigma_1\)

Let \(\rho=\|m\|^2/W_2^2(\mu,\nu)\in[0,1]\) be the shift fraction. Then:

$$\frac{W_2^2-\mathrm{SW}_2^2}{W_2^2}\;\geq\;\Bigl(1-\tfrac{1}{d}\Bigr)\rho$$

The bound tightens as \(\rho\to1\) (pure shift) and holds for all \(d,\Sigma_0,\Sigma_1\).

Anisotropic experiment: sweep covariance condition number \(\kappa\). Measured deficit (5 seeds) stays above \((1-1/d)\rho\) bound. Validated \(\kappa\in[1,50]\).

Deficit tracks \(1-1/d\) for any covariance; error bars \(\leq0.03\).
The bound is structural, not statistical — more projections cannot help; the deficit floor comes from integrating over the sphere.

Theorem 2 — Certification is provably hard

Theorem 2 — \(\Omega(n^2)\) Certification Barrier

In the entry-query model, any (randomized) algorithm that outputs a valid certified gap \(G\leq\delta\) for every \(C\in[0,1]^{n\times n}\) (with uniform marginals, \(\delta<\tfrac{1}{4}\)) must make \(\Omega(n^2)\) cost evaluations.

Proof sketch — planted-matching adversary:

  1. Answer every queried entry = 1
  2. If \(q < n^2/16\): Hall's theorem guarantees a permutation \(\sigma\) avoiding all \(q\) queried positions in the bipartite complement
  3. Plant \(C[i,\sigma(i)]=0\); all others = 1 \(\Rightarrow\) \(\mathrm{OT}^\star=0\)
  4. Algorithm sees only 1's — cannot certify \(G\leq\delta\)
  5. Yao's minimax principle extends to randomized algorithms

escape Structured costs

Sliced (\(O(n)\) projections), low-rank (\(O(nr)\)), entropic Sinkhorn (\(O(n^2)\) but GPU-fast) — exploit \(c\)-structure to escape the adversary. Structure is not a convenience; it is a necessity.

observed Memory wall in practice

On an 85 GB A100 (measured):
Float64: OOM at \(n=55{,}000\) (~17 GB matrix)
Float32: OOM at \(n=80{,}000\) (~25 GB matrix)

The \(\Omega(n^2)\) barrier is not just theoretical — it is the GPU memory wall.

General certification is fundamentally \(\Omega(n^2)\). Any sub-quadratic certified method must exploit structure (sliced, low-rank, entropic) to avoid the adversary.

A constructive recipe: Composed Gap Certificate

Plug any lower-bound oracle and any upper-bound oracle into a single framework — get a valid certified bracket at near-linear cost.

Cost

Both oracles run independently. Total cost \(=\mathrm{cost}(\mathcal{L})+\mathrm{cost}(\mathcal{U})\). Both are near-linear in \(n\) → avoids the \(\Omega(n^2)\) barrier (Thm 2) via structure.

Calibration

Projections \(\uparrow\) tightens \(L\); batch size \(m\uparrow\) tightens \(U\); rank \(r\uparrow\) tightens \(U\). Plug-and-play: swap oracles without changing the certificate validity.

Failure mode & fix

If \(\mathcal{L}=\)Sliced, the structural floor (Thm 1) limits tightening. Fix: upgrade \(\mathcal{L}\) to margin-corrected neural (Lemma 1) or entropic lower bound.

Composition is the key insight: two cheap one-sided estimates, run independently, produce a valid two-sided certificate without \(O(n^2)\) dense computation.

Lemma 1 — Neural OT is certifiable up to a margin

Trained neural potentials are generally not dual-feasible — but a computable correction restores validity.

Lemma 1 — Feasibility margin

Let \(f_\theta,g_\phi\) be trained neural potentials. Define the violation margin:

$$\varepsilon_\text{viol}:=\sup_{x\in\mathrm{supp}\,\mu,\,y\in\mathrm{supp}\,\nu}\bigl(f_\theta(x)+g_\phi(y)-c(x,y)\bigr)_+$$

Then \(L_\text{corr}:=\int f_\theta\,d\mu+\int g_\phi\,d\nu-\varepsilon_\text{viol}\leq\mathrm{OT}^\star\). With any feasible coupling \(U\):

$$G_\text{corr}=U-L_\text{corr}\;\text{ is a valid certified gap.}$$

What this means

Neural OT, previously "uncertified," becomes certifiable. The correction is exact: \(G_\text{corr}\) exceeds the raw neural gap by exactly \(\varepsilon_\text{viol}\), which measures how much the potentials violate the \(c\)-transform constraint.

In practice

\(\varepsilon_\text{viol}\) is estimated on a finite subsample of \(\mathrm{supp}\,\mu\times\mathrm{supp}\,\nu\). A well-trained ICNN (width 128, 4000 iters) achieves small \(\varepsilon_\text{viol}\), giving a tight certified bracket.

This adds a certified neural marker to the fidelity–cost frontier.

Lemma 1 bridges theory and practice: any approximate dual solver can be turned into a valid lower bound oracle for the CGC algorithm by computing \(\varepsilon_\text{viol}\).

Which proxy is a valid bound for \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\)?

Only the duality gap \(G\) is guaranteed to be a valid two-sided bound — without ever computing \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\).

What "valid bound" means

A proxy \(P\) is a valid bound in a setting if \(P\leq U-\mathrm{OT}^\star\) (i.e., it lower-bounds the primal excess). Gap \(G\) satisfies this by construction — always. Sinkhorn divergence \(S_\varepsilon\) satisfies it 79% of the time (correlation but not guarantee).

All proxies correlate — only \(G\) certifies

Primal excess, dual deficit, and Sinkhorn-div all have Spearman \(\rho\geq0.93\) with true error. But primal/dual excess need \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\) to compute. Only \(G\) is computable and valid.

Gap \(G\) is 100% valid as a two-sided bound across all tested settings. Correlation is not enough — certification requires the guarantee.

How gap-tol works

Sweep \(\varepsilon\); select the largest \(\varepsilon\) such that relative gap \(G/U\) falls below a threshold \(\tau\). No labels, no validation set, no domain knowledge.

Negative result — over-sharpening

On MNIST→USPS, continuing to shrink \(\varepsilon\) past the optimal point degrades accuracy by −6.6 pts. Gap-tol detects the saturation point; fixed \(\varepsilon\) and sinkdiv-min do not.

The gap is necessary but not sufficient — it warns before the task metric does.

Gap-tol = oracle on both tasks. It is the only label-free rule that consistently finds the accuracy-maximizing regularization strength.

Summary

We propose \(G=U-L\) as the missing coordinate unifying approximate OT — and prove it is both structurally limited and cheaply computable.

concept

\(G=U-L\) as a cross-family, computable fidelity coordinate. Never needs \(\mathrm{OT}^\star\); certifies both primal and dual error.

theory

Thm 1: sliced deficit \(\geq1-1/d\) for any Gaussian family — structural, not statistical.
Thm 2: certifying \(G\leq\delta\) costs \(\Omega(n^2)\) in general.

algorithm

CGC: compose any \(\mathcal{L}+\mathcal{U}\) for a valid certificate at near-linear cost.
Lemma 1: neural OT certifiable via \(\varepsilon_\text{viol}\) margin correction.

evidence

Gap 100% valid; gap-tol = oracle (MNIST 0.62, scRNA 0.95); deficit tracks \(1-1/d\) in PCA, diffmap, raw gene space.

The duality gap converts approximate OT from a collection of heuristics into a certifiable, comparable science — with clear structural limits and a constructive recipe to work around them.
